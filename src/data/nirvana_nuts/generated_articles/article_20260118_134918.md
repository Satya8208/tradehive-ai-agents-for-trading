# Dying is an Art, Just Like Living Is

You practice living every day—optimizing mornings, building habits, chasing meaning. But dying? That's supposed to just happen to you. **What if that's the biggest delusion we carry?** Sylvia Plath said dying is an art. She wasn't being poetic. She was telling the truth most people spend their whole lives avoiding.

## The Art You Refuse to Learn

Living gets all the workshops. The seminars. The self-help books and morning routines and productivity hacks.

Dying gets silence.

**You'll spend decades perfecting how to live and zero minutes preparing for the one thing that's actually guaranteed.** The average person thinks about their death for less than 3 minutes per year, according to research from the University of Kentucky. Meanwhile, you'll spend 4 hours deciding what to watch on Netflix tonight.

This isn't morbid. This is practical.

**Every artist studies their medium.** Painters learn color theory. Musicians learn scales. You're performing both life and death whether you practice or not. Only one of these performances happens without a rehearsal.

The Stoics knew this. Marcus Aurelius wrote about death in his private journals more than he wrote about victory. Not because he was depressed—because **he understood that the frame around the painting determines how you see the art inside it.**

Here's what you do with this: Spend 10 minutes this week writing your own eulogy. Not the one you want others to give. The one that's true if you died tonight. The gap between those two documents is your actual work.

## The Performance Anxiety of Existence

Living is improvisation. Dying is the closing monologue.

**You get anxious about presentations at work but not about the final presentation of your entire existence.** That's not wisdom. That's avoidance wearing the mask of being "present."

Bronnie Ware, a palliative care nurse, documented the top 5 regrets of the dying over decades of bedside conversations. Number one? "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me."

**They figured out the art of living only when they were graded on their death.**

The art isn't in the dying itself—it's in living in a way that makes dying coherent. When your death makes sense as the final sentence of your story, you've mastered both arts. When it doesn't, you've been performing someone else's script.

Most people treat life like a rough draft they'll revise later. There is no later. **This is the final copy, and you're writing it in pen.**

What you do: List three things you're doing because you "should" and one thing you're not doing because you're afraid. The death-bed version of you already knows which one matters. Listen to that person.

## The Technique: Subtraction as Mastery

Every art form is about knowing what to remove.

Michelangelo said he didn't create David—he just removed everything that wasn't David from the marble. **Your life is the same block of stone.** Most of what you're doing isn't you. It's debris.

The average person has 3.7 close friends by age 65, down from 6 in their twenties (Survey Center on American Life, 2021). You're not losing friends randomly. **You're slowly learning who matters.** That's the art of subtraction.

Dying forces the ultimate edit. Everything non-essential gets cut. The question isn't "What do you want to do before you die?" It's "What are you doing now that you'd cut if you knew you had six months?"

That meeting. That grudge. That project you're doing for someone else's approval. That relationship held together by history instead of presence.

**The art of dying well is practicing those cuts before you're forced to make them.**

Steve Jobs, in his Stanford speech, said remembering he would die soon was the most important tool he ever had for making big choices. Not because it made him reckless—because **it made him honest.**

What you do: Write down everything you did last week. Cross out anything you wouldn't do if you had one year left. What's left is your actual life. Everything else is rehearsal for a play you're not starring in.

## The Audience Problem

You're performing your life for people who won't remember most of it.

**The average funeral has 50-75 attendees.** Of those, maybe 10 will think about you monthly after a year. Maybe 3 after five years. You're optimizing your entire existence for an audience that dissolves.

This isn't sad. This is liberating.

**If almost no one will remember, you're free to perform for yourself.** The art of living becomes internal. The art of dying becomes about whether you're satisfied with the performance, not whether it gets applause.

Ernest Becker won a Pulitzer for "The Denial of Death," arguing that civilization itself is a massive, elaborate defense mechanism against mortality. We build monuments. We chase fame. We have kids and name buildings after ourselves.

All of it is **screaming into the void, hoping the void screams back.**

But here's the thing about art: the best art doesn't beg for attention. It exists because it must. Your life can be the same.

Van Gogh sold one painting while alive. One. His art wasn't for his audience—it was for the act itself. **He mastered dying unknown and living anyway.**

What you do: Name three things you're doing primarily for external validation. Not networking or career stuff that's strategic—the things you do because you need someone to witness them. Stop one of them this month. Notice if your life gets worse or better.

## The Final Brushstroke

Plath called dying an art "like everything else." Not special. Not sacred. Just another skill in the repertoire of being human.

**You don't get extra points for pretending you're immortal.** You get a worse painting.

The Japanese have a concept: "shi-ni-gao"—your "death face." Not literal—it's the expression of how you meet your end. Peaceful? Regretful? Surprised? **Your whole life is practicing for that face.**

A study from Stanford's Center on Longevity found that people who think about death regularly make better long-term decisions, have stronger relationships, and report higher life satisfaction. Not despite thinking about death. **Because of it.**

The frame sharpens the painting.

When you accept that dying is part of the performance, living stops being a frantic rehearsal. You stop saving the good china for special occasions. You stop waiting for permission. You stop performing for the cheap seats.

**You start treating each day like a brushstroke that matters because the canvas isn't infinite.**

Here's the paradox nobody tells you: the more you practice dying, the better you get at living. Not because you become reckless—because you become intentional.

What you do: Set a daily reminder on your phone. Just two words: "Memento mori." Remember death. Not to be afraid. To be awake.

## What Mastery Actually Looks Like

You'll know you're getting good at both arts when you stop separating them.

Living well and dying well aren't two different skills. **They're the same performance viewed from different angles.**

The person who masters this doesn't fear death—they just refuse to waste life. They don't become morbid. They become present. They don't give up on ambition. They give up on pretending.

**They stop rehearsing and start performing.**

Oliver Sacks, the neurologist, wrote an op-ed at 81 when he learned he had terminal cancer. He said he didn't have time for anything inessential anymore. No time for disputes. No time for negativity. Only time for what was real.

He'd been practicing his whole life. The diagnosis just made the practice visible.

That's what mastery looks like. Not waiting for the deadline to figure out what matters. **Treating every day like it's both the rough draft and the final copy.**

Because it is.

What you do: Tonight, before bed, ask yourself one question: "If this was my last day, did I perform it well?" Not perfectly. Not productively. Well. As in, true to what matters. Keep asking until the answer is yes more often than no.

---

Plath was right. Dying is an art. So is living. The secret nobody tells you? **You're already performing both.** The only question is whether you're doing it on purpose or by accident. Most people sleepwalk through the gallery of their own life and wonder why nothing looks like them. You have one canvas. Every day you're either adding brushstrokes that matter or covering up the real painting with someone else's colors. Which one are you doing today?
